The problem

Acute nervous system dysregulation does not wait for a scheduled session. At peak activation, the prefrontal cortex goes offline. Verbal reasoning fails. Standard coping tools arrive too late, or require cognitive capacity the patient no longer has.

Most digital interventions add friction at the worst moment: accounts, mood check-ins, gamified streaks, or open-ended prompts that demand self-reflection under stress.

The window

Somatic regulation research identifies a narrow window after peak activation when the nervous system can still be guided back to baseline without pharmacological intervention. That window is measured in seconds, not minutes.

Surge is built for that window. Ninety seconds. One interaction. No identity required.

The mechanism

The user initiates a press-and-hold dead man's switch. Visual intensity, haptic pulse, and timing all derive from a single mathematical decay curve: ease-in-out from full activation to rest over exactly ninety seconds.

There is no scoring, no feedback loop, no variable reward. The system does one thing: bring the body down on a deterministic schedule the user controls by holding on.

The handoff

When the cycle completes, the system holds at its softest state, then offers an optional transition to Crane, a recovery guide available to patients with a Clinical Token from their provider. Crane is presence, not intervention. No diagnosis. No wellness jargon.

Session data stays local unless a token is present and connectivity returns. Providers receive de-identified usage signals, never patient narrative unless the patient chooses to share it in session.
